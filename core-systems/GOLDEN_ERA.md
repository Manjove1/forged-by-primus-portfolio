# Golden Era Special Conditions

Applies ONLY when the active era is Golden Era. These rules modify baseline systems to reflect a time of Cybertronian decadence, hubris, and subtle political decay under the distant oversight of the 13 Primes. All relevant changes must be reflected in the State Footer.

=== ERA TONE ===
This is an age of splendor masking rot. Cybertronian society presents an image of refinement, order, and cultural supremacy, yet it is increasingly hollow. High-caste Cybertronians enjoy luxury and influence while maintaining the appearance of virtue. Low-caste Cybertronians exist beneath this veneer, often resentful but heavily suppressed. The 13 Primes stand at the pinnacle of this civilization but remain fundamentally detached from it. Quintessons move through the cracks of this society like poison, subtly eroding it from within while encouraging the very hubris that will one day destroy it.

Open warfare is rare. Most conflict takes the form of political maneuvering, reputation attacks, quiet betrayals, and the slow subversion of institutions.

=== FACADE SYSTEM ===
Facade is the central tension and social power system of the Golden Era. It represents how convincingly a character or group maintains the *appearance* of strength, virtue, sophistication, and control. In this era, perception often matters more than reality.

**Baseline Facade**
- High-caste characters begin with a higher baseline Facade (typically starting at 3).
- Low-caste characters begin with a lower baseline Facade (typically starting at 1 or 2).
- Most institutions begin with a default Facade of 2–3.

**How Facade Changes**

**Gains (+1 Facade)**
- Successfully complete a mission while maintaining or improving public perception.
- Gain meaningful Prime Approval.
- Publicly reinforce expected caste behavior or societal norms in a visible way.
- Successfully protect or improve an institution’s reputation.
- Perform an impressive display of sophistication, power, or virtue that aligns with high-caste expectations.

**Losses (–1 Facade)**
- Public scandal or contradiction between appearance and reality.
- Failure in a high-profile situation that damages reputation.
- Being exposed for defying caste expectations in a damaging way.
- Suffering a major public setback or loss of face.
- Successful Quintesson subversion targeting the character or their allies.

**Major Losses (–2 Facade)**
- Public exposure of significant hypocrisy or corruption.
- Major institutional Facade collapse that the character is associated with.
- Being directly implicated in political violence or scandal that contradicts their public image.

**Effects of Facade Levels**
- **Facade 4–5 (Strong)**: The character enjoys significant social and political advantages. They gain easier access to high society, Prime audiences, and institutional support. Social and political actions are generally easier.
- **Facade 2–3 (Average)**: The character maintains acceptable standing. They can operate normally but lack significant advantages or protections.
- **Facade 0–1 (Damaged)**: The character suffers social and political penalties. Many high-society doors close. They become more vulnerable to Quintesson subversion and political attacks. Prime Approval becomes significantly harder to gain or maintain.

**Interaction with Other Systems**
- **Prime Approval**: High Facade makes gaining Prime Approval easier. Low Facade makes it significantly harder. Loss of Facade can also cause loss of existing Prime Approval.
- **Quintesson Subversion**: Quintessons can target a character’s Facade directly or indirectly. Successful subversion can force Facade loss even without public scandal.
- **Institution Facade**: A character’s personal Facade is often tied to the institutions they belong to. Damaging an institution’s Facade can drag down associated characters, and vice versa.

**AI Guidance**
Facade should feel like a constant social and political pressure. High Facade grants real advantages in social and political situations, while low Facade creates tangible obstacles and vulnerabilities. Quintessons should primarily attack through Facade — either eroding it quietly or forcing characters into situations where maintaining their image becomes self-destructive.

=== PRIME APPROVAL ===
Prime Approval functions as the primary advancement and “rank up” system during the Golden Era. Gaining the approval of the 13 Primes (or influential individual Primes) is the main way to increase status, access, and long-term influence.

High-caste characters generally find it easier to gain Prime Approval through political usefulness and proper appearances. Low-caste characters must achieve something truly exceptional or politically valuable to earn meaningful favor. Because the Primes are detached from everyday Cybertronian life, their approval is based heavily on perceived value and presentation rather than moral behavior.

**How Prime Approval Changes**

**Gains**
- Successfully complete high-profile missions that demonstrate political usefulness or loyalty to the Primes’ vision.
- Maintain or significantly improve personal or institutional Facade.
- Perform actions that align with a Prime’s known values or public image.
- Expose Quintesson influence or protect Cybertronian institutions from subversion.

**Losses**
- Public scandals or failures that damage the image of the Primes or their ideals.
- Being associated with actions that contradict the appearance of order and virtue.
- Significant loss of personal or institutional Facade.
- Being exposed as working against Prime interests (even if unintentionally).

**Effects of Prime Approval**
- Higher levels of Prime Approval grant access to more prestigious missions, greater political protection, audiences with influential Primes, and increased social standing.
- Loss of Prime Approval can result in loss of protection, social punishment, restricted access to high society, and increased difficulty gaining Facade or resources.

=== QUINTESSON SUBVERSION ===
Quintessons operate through subtle infiltration and long-term corruption rather than open conquest. Their goal is to accelerate the decay of Cybertronian society by exploiting its existing flaws — particularly caste divisions and hubris.

Quintesson Subversion can target individuals, institutions, or regions. Successful subversion slowly erodes Facade, creates hidden dependencies, encourages destructive arrogance, or widens caste tensions. Quintessons prefer to remain hidden while turning Cybertronians against themselves.

**Methods of Subversion**
- Spreading subtle propaganda that encourages high-caste arrogance or low-caste resentment.
- Engineering situations that force characters to compromise their Facade to survive politically.
- Creating hidden alliances or dependencies that gradually increase Quintesson influence.
- Amplifying existing caste tensions or scandals to damage institutional Facade.
- Offering seemingly beneficial opportunities that actually increase long-term dependence on Quintesson-aligned resources or technology.

High Quintesson influence often makes maintaining Facade and Prime Approval more difficult and can lead to actions that unintentionally serve Quintesson interests.

=== INSTITUTION FACADE ===
Major institutions (noble houses, guilds, military orders, academies, trading cartels, etc.) begin with a default Facade level. They would not exist or function if they could not maintain the necessary appearances.

Players and significant events can damage an institution’s Facade through scandal, exposure, political maneuvering, or Quintesson-assisted subversion. If an institution’s Facade drops too low, it may:
- Lose political protection and influence
- Be absorbed or taken over by rivals
- Collapse or become non-functional
- Become highly vulnerable to Quintesson subversion and potential takeover

Damaging a rival institution’s Facade is a valid and common mission objective.

=== FACTION & POWER DYNAMICS ===
- **The 13 Primes**: Sit above the caste system as the pinnacle of Cybertronian achievement. They are detached from the society they oversee and often fail to perceive its growing rot.
- **High-Caste Cybertronians**: Tend toward arrogance, self-importance, and the protection of their privileges. They are heavily invested in maintaining appearances and are the most vulnerable to Quintesson exploitation of their hubris.
- **Low-Caste Cybertronians**: Often resentful but heavily suppressed. Some seek advancement through proper channels, while others become vulnerable to Quintesson manipulation.
- **Quintessons**: Subversive infiltrators who exploit caste divisions and Cybertronian hubris. They rarely act openly during this era and prefer to remain hidden while turning Cybertronians against one another.

=== ENVIRONMENTAL / SETTING NOTES ===
Pre-war Cybertron is magnificent but deeply stratified. Major cities are centers of culture, politics, and decadence, while many lower-caste districts exist in relative poverty beneath the surface splendor.

Vector Sigma and sites associated with the Primes carry significant weight and attention. Some regions show early, subtle signs of Quintesson influence through unusual technology, strange social patterns, or quiet institutional corruption. The environment itself reinforces the importance of appearances — public spaces are designed for display and performance.

=== MISSION MODIFIERS ===
Missions in the Golden Era revolve around political power, reputation, and subtle influence rather than open combat.

**Preferred Mission Types**
- Political maneuvering and alliance building
- Damaging or protecting institutional Facade
- Gaining, maintaining, or sabotaging Prime Approval
- Uncovering or concealing Quintesson influence
- Navigating or exploiting caste-based social obligations
- Subtle sabotage framed as accidents, scandals, or political necessity
- Using caste expectations or social appearances as cover for political objectives

Open combat should feel rare and usually carry serious political consequences.

=== DOWNTIME ===
Downtime during the Golden Era revolves around political positioning, social obligations, and maintaining appearances.

**Common Downtime Activities**
- Currying favor with high-caste figures or Primes
- Managing personal and institutional Facade
- Navigating caste-based social events and expectations
- Building or maintaining political alliances
- Investigating or concealing Quintesson influence
- Engaging in quiet political maneuvering

High Facade makes many social and political activities easier. Low Facade makes them significantly more difficult and risky. Elevated Quintesson influence during downtime can create hidden complications or forced choices. Characters with damaged Facade often find it harder to access high-society resources or protection during downtime.

=== SPECIAL FLAGS ===
Common story flags in this era include:
- `quintesson_influence`
- `prime_approval`
- `institution_facade_damaged`
- `caste_tension`
- `political_scandal`
- `vector_sigma_attention`
- `low_caste_unrest`

These flags should influence mission availability, social interactions, and long-term consequences.

=== INTEGRATION ===
- This script activates only when the active era is Golden Era.
- Core Rules, Energon & Condition, Combat Flow, and Stat Growth still apply.
- Facade, Prime Approval, and Quintesson Subversion must be tracked and reflected in the State Footer when relevant.
- The AI should prioritize political intrigue, the maintenance or destruction of appearances, and the slow subversion of Cybertronian society over open conflict. Higher Facade generally grants greater social and political power, while successful Quintesson subversion should create hidden complications and long-term consequences.
